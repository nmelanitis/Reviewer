## Final review section

Across the extracted literature, the clearest confirmed downstream uses of the target-paper/model family cluster around three patterns: **direct architectural extension**, **cross-domain adaptation**, and **benchmarking against the original approach or its close variants**. The strongest evidence comes from papers that explicitly name the target family and modify its core pretraining or representation-learning scheme, rather than merely mentioning generic pretraining or transformer backbones.

A recurring theme is the **adaptation of masked autoencoding / masked image modeling to new modalities and tasks**. Several papers are explicit extensions of MAE-style learning rather than indirect references. For example, **BEVT** applies masked image modeling first to images and then jointly with masked video modeling, making it a direct video-domain extension of the masked-autoencoding family. Likewise, **PiMAE**, **scMMAE**, **A masked autoencoder-based three-dimensional foundation model for vortex identification**, **fNIRS Foundation Model for Few-Shot Based fNIRS Classification**, **Quaternion-based vision-transformer for polycrystalline EBSD scans pre-trained on large-scale synthetic data**, **AV-MaskEnhancer**, **RamanMAE**, and **Echo-VideoMAE / Echo-Vision-FM** all use MAE or MAE-like pretraining as the central modeling strategy and then adapt it to domain-specific inputs such as microscopy, omics, materials science, physiology, spectroscopy, and echocardiography. In each case, the target model is not merely cited as background; it is instantiated as the basis of the proposed method, typically with modified masking schemes, encoders, or decoders, followed by downstream benchmarking.

Another confirmed pattern is **parameter-efficient or backbone-level adaptation of pretrained foundation models**. **Integrating Spatial Prior Adapter for Enhancing SAM Performance in Medical Image Segmentation** explicitly treats SAM as a base model and introduces a frozen-encoder adapter to improve medical segmentation. **FocSAM** similarly modifies SAM with architectural changes aimed at difficult interactive segmentation cases. These papers are useful for the review because they show that the target family is being used as a transferable foundation model, but also because they demonstrate that later work often emphasizes lightweight adaptation rather than full retraining. A related case is **Cellpose-SAM**, which adapts the Cellpose pipeline by replacing the backbone with a transformer-based variant and then benchmarks whether this improves cellular segmentation; the reported conclusion is cautious and suggests that the architectural swap does not necessarily outperform the original Cellpose setup.

The literature also contains several **clear benchmarking and reevaluation studies**. **Benchmarking cellular segmentation methods against Cellpose** is explicitly centered on Cellpose as a target benchmark model and tests whether a transformer-augmented variant improves performance; the answer appears to be negative under the authors’ revised evaluation. More broadly, papers such as **Scaling Self-Supervised Learning for Histopathology with Masked Image Modeling** and its follow-up benchmarking discussion provide a strong example of the target family serving as a scaling reference: iBOT-based masked image modeling is compared against prior SSL methods and evaluated across architecture size, data scale, and transfer settings. Similarly, **A Framework for Evaluating the Efficacy of Foundation Embedding Models in Healthcare** uses Google’s Derm Foundation embeddings as the focal model and benchmarks them against strong vision baselines and CLIP-style alternatives. These studies are important because they show the target models being assessed not only for absolute performance, but also for robustness, fairness, and sensitivity to dataset shift.

A smaller set of papers appear to be **adjacent rather than directly dependent** on the target model family. For instance, some works mention pretraining, masking, or transformer architectures without providing enough evidence of explicit MAE/SAM/target-model reuse; these should be treated cautiously. Similarly, several chapter- or survey-style sources contextualize the field by listing related models, but they do not provide direct experimental evidence of downstream adoption. In a literature review, these are best used as **background framing** rather than as confirmed examples of reuse.

Taken together, the confirmed evidence suggests that the target paper/model family has been used most often as a **generalizable pretraining paradigm**: later work either (i) **moves the same masked-reconstruction logic into a new domain**, (ii) **wraps a pretrained foundation model with an adaptation module**, or (iii) **benchmarks a new method against the original or a close implementation of it**. The strongest and most defensible synthesis is therefore that the target family has become a reusable design template for downstream representation learning, especially in domains where labeled data are scarce and transfer performance matters.
